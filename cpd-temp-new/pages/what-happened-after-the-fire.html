What Happened After the Fire?

The Government Response

The government response immediately after the fire has been heavily scrutinised. It is not only the cause of the fire being questioned but also how the local councils and government responded.

Source: The government responded swiftly to 7/7 bombings. What went wrong at Grenfell Tower? (The Guardian)

The Guardian article frames the aftermath of Grenfell as a failure of response as well as a failure of prevention. For teaching purposes, the key question is not only how the fire happened, but how public bodies responded to people who had lost relatives, homes, documents, medicines and a sense of safety. The early lack of clear information, visible leadership and coordinated support became part of the injustice experienced by the community.

7/7 Bombings (2005)

The London bombings of 7 July 2005 provide a useful point of comparison. Within two days, emergency planners had agreed to create a dedicated family assistance centre, drawing on lessons from earlier disasters and giving survivors and relatives a clearer place to seek information, practical help and emotional support.

The centre first opened at the Queen Mother Sports Centre on Vauxhall Bridge Road and later moved to the Horticultural Halls in Vincent Square. It brought together the police, Westminster City Council and voluntary organisations, including the British Red Cross, and remained in operation for several weeks.

A relief fund and telephone support line were also put in place early in the response. The arrangements were not perfect, but they show what a more planned, visible and joined-up public response can look like when agencies work from a shared emergency framework.

Grenfell Tower Fire (2017)

By contrast, many people affected by Grenfell described having to search for basic information and assistance while community organisations, faith groups, charities and volunteers filled gaps left by official bodies. The comparison helps learners ask why an emergency response can be experienced as abandonment by the very people it is meant to serve.
