Bringing a Community Together

The Community Response

In stark contrast to the slow and disorganised official response, the community around Grenfell Tower mobilised almost immediately. Neighbours, voluntary organisations, local businesses, and religious communities of all faiths stepped in to provide shelter, food, clothing, emotional support, and practical help — often within hours of the fire.

The Role of Faith Communities

Churches, mosques, and other places of worship in North Kensington were among the first to open their doors. They offered space for displaced families, organised donations, and provided pastoral care in the days and weeks that followed.

A report by the Christian thinktank Theos found that faith groups responded “rapidly, compassionately and holistically” to the disaster. The report, After Grenfell: The Faith Groups’ Response, examines how these communities organised relief efforts and argues that places of worship should practise emergency responses before crises occur — what the report calls “preparation in peacetime.”

The Al-Manaar mosque, just yards from the tower, became one of the most important hubs in the immediate aftermath — receiving and distributing donations, hosting community meetings, and providing a safe space for grieving families.

Solidarity Beyond North Kensington

The response to Grenfell did not stop at the boundaries of North Kensington. Across the UK and around the world, people and organisations were moved to act — not as a brief outpouring of sympathy, but through sustained relationships of solidarity that continue years later.

Cornwall Hugs Grenfell is one of the most remarkable examples. Founded just six days after the fire, the charity has provided over 500 respite holidays in Cornwall for survivors, bereaved families, and firefighters — bridging two communities more than 300 miles apart. Thousands of Cornish individuals, businesses, and organisations have contributed, and the bond between Cornwall and Kensington has become a lasting symbol of grassroots compassion. In 2019, a street sign in the fishing village of Mousehole — on a 200-year-old lane already called Grenfell Street — became the first in the UK to carry the Grenfell green heart emblem, a permanent marker of the partnership between the two communities.

Grenfell’s significance has also been recognised internationally. In South Korea, the lessons of Grenfell are taught at the 4.16 Institute for Life and Safety Education, an educational institution established by bereaved families following the Sewol ferry disaster of April 2014, in which 304 people died — including 250 school students from Danwon High School. The Institute features preserved ‘memory classrooms’ and offers educational programmes built around the principles of remembrance, empathy, and democratic citizenship. Researchers at the Institute and the University of Southampton have collaborated to explore how disaster education can connect educators and affected communities across national borders, using Grenfell and Sewol as parallel case studies in learning from preventable tragedy.

These examples illustrate a wider truth about Grenfell: the disaster created connections that endure, not only in grief but in a shared commitment to justice, safety, and the belief that ordinary people can hold institutions to account.
